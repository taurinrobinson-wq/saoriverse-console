1. Breach of fiduciary duty by Pak relating to alleged theft of clients 
Part of plaintiffs' breach of fiduciary duty claim against Pak rests on the unsupportable 
factual contention that Pak used fraud and deceit to steal existing clients and potential new 
clients from Brown so that she could go into active competition with him when she was still 
purporting to work as his office manager. The court agrees that had Pak transferred Brown's 
cases, solicited the clients to switch fim1s, purchased new leads for Norris -all without Brown's 
knowledge and consent -she would clearly have breached her fiduciary duty to Brown. But the 
evidence does not support plaintiffs ' version of events. As discussed above, the court finds that 
Baird Brown voluntarily withdrew from many of his cases, encourag ed Pak to work with Norris 
to create a new mass tort practice for Norris's own firm, agreed to the substitution of Norris as 
counsel of record on his existing cases, and consented to her using his office with Norris to 
create a new practice, while wrapping up Brown's few remaining personal injury cases. Having 
consented to Pak playing this dual role, Brown cannot claim she breached a fiduciary duty to hi 
and his firm.
