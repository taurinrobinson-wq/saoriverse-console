ORIGINAL (1 paragraph):

"Defendant Michelle Pak moves to vacate the judgment under Code of Civil 
Procedure section 663 because the judgment entered is not supported by the 
Court's own factual findings and rests on incorrect legal conclusions. For 
purposes of this motion only, the Statement of Decision ("SOD") is accepted 
as written. The issue under Section 663 is not whether the Court should 
revisit its factual determinations, but whether the legal conclusions and 
judgment follow those findings. Here, they do not.

The SOD's factual findings negate essential elements of elder financial 
abuse, breach of fiduciary duty, and damages. Section 663 exists precisely 
for cases like this, where the Statement of Decision is extensive, but the 
judgment does not follow from the findings made. The Court found that Brown 
was competent in 2019, had full access to the accounts, reviewed the 
transactions, raised no objections, knew the leads were not being purchased 
for his firm, and consented to the transfer of clients to Norris. The SOD 
also found that Ms. Pak funded the vast majority of the 2019 lead purchases, 
and 'may have believed' she was acting within the parties' long-standing 
reimbursement structure. These findings cannot support the judgment entered. 
Furthermore, several of the SOD's principal conclusions are presented with 
qualifications or ambiguity, such as the Court's observations that Pak 'may 
have believed' the transactions were authorized and that her actions were 
'at most' negligent. Under Code of Civil Procedure section 634, when an 
ambiguity in the Statement of Decision is raised, no implied finding may be 
inferred in favor of the prevailing party. Because the judgment is 
inconsistent with and unsupported by the findings made, it must be vacated 
or modified."

REVISED VERSION (0.75 pages):
──────────────────────────────
"A. Plaintiffs' Sole 'Fact' Is Not a Factual Finding—It Is an Unsupported 
Conclusion

Plaintiffs' opposition argues that Defendant "failed to address" the SOD's 
statement that "Brown did not know that Pak was purchasing leads for the 
Norris firm as opposed to the Baird firm." But this sentence is not a 
factual finding; it is a legal conclusion about Brown's mental state. 
Section 663 requires the judgment to be supported by the Court's factual 
findings, not by isolated conclusions.

The surrounding SOD text contains no factual findings establishing this 
conclusion. The Court states only that it "could not find a reasonable 
explanation for the source of funds" for certain transactions. That is not 
a finding of disloyalty, concealment, or wrongful taking. It is a 
documentation gap. The SOD further states that "Pak had full authority and 
access to both of those accounts pursuant to her agreement with Brown," 
that "Brown was writing checks from the same two accounts in 2019 and so 
would have been aware of these transactions," and that "Brown made no 
objection." (SOD, p. 27:7-10.) None of this supports the conclusion that 
Brown lacked knowledge.

More importantly, the SOD contains multiple factual findings that 
affirmatively contradict Plaintiffs' conclusion:
• Brown was competent and handling his business affairs during the relevant 
  period
• Brown knew he was winding down his practice and facing suspension
• Brown knew the leads were not being purchased for him because he was 
  stepping back from practice
• Brown consented to Norris taking over his cases
• Brown introduced Norris to staff as the attorney taking over
• Brown reviewed the transactions and raised no objection
• Brown participated in the transition of his practice
• The Court found that the May 2019 leads were purchased for Brown and only 
  later identified as for Norris after Brown agreed to the transition

These are factual findings. They negate the conclusion Plaintiffs rely on. 
A judgment cannot rest on a conclusion unsupported by findings, especially 
where the findings affirmatively contradict it. Because Plaintiffs point to 
no factual finding establishing lack of knowledge, disloyalty, wrongful 
taking, or undue influence, their argument fails at the threshold."

"C. Mrs. Brown's Testimony—Plaintiffs' Strongest Evidence—Was Expressly

Plaintiffs rely on testimony from Mrs. Brown that Mr. Brown told her the 
lead purchases were a good investment. But the Statement of Decision

'Mrs. Brown also claims that she was aware that the Brown firm was

investment.... The court found this testimony unlikely, given that her 
testimony was imprecise and she admitted that at this same time her husband 
was not telling her the truth about one key issue—that he was under 
investigation by the state bar and facing a one-year suspension.' (SOD, 
pp. 11:5-22.)

The Court's credibility determination was unambiguous: this testimony is 
'unlikely' and 'imprecise.' The SOD further found that Mrs. Brown was 
unaware of:
• Her husband's State Bar investigation and suspension (hidden for three 
  years)
• His transfer of cases to Norris
• His winding down of practice
• The Norris relationship and fee agreements
• The Roh case substitution
• The status of the Mick case and other professional details

This is not a gap in Mrs. Brown's knowledge. It is a pattern of 
concealment. The Court recognized that Brown routinely hid major 
professional facts from his wife, which directly undermines any reliance 
on her testimony about his knowledge of the lead transactions.

Critically, the Court used Mrs. Brown's lack of knowledge to support the 
opposite conclusion Plaintiffs want. The SOD states: 'This further supports 
the court's conclusion that Brown knew he needed another attorney to handle 
cases.' (SOD, p. 28:22-23.) In other words, the fact that Brown concealed 
the transition from his wife demonstrates that Brown understood the 
transition was occurring—he was managing the information flow. Her 
testimony does not establish Brown's actual knowledge; it establishes that 
Brown controlled what she knew.

A judgment cannot rest on testimony the Court expressly found not credible. 
Plaintiffs' reliance on testimony the Court rejected underscores that the 
judgment is unsupported by the Court's actual findings."

SECTION VI.B: MAJOR REVISION (RENT DAMAGES)
======================================================================

ORIGINAL (~0.75 pages):
───────────────────────
"B. The Findings Do Not Support the $24,000 Rent Award

The SOD's findings regarding rent likewise negate causation, harm, and 
liability. The Court found that Brown made no rent payments after April 
2020 and benefitted from Ms. Pak's continued use of the office to wind down 
his remaining cases. (SOD, pp. 11:5–22; 23:23–25.) A plaintiff who pays 
nothing cannot recover rent he did not pay. The SOD also found that Ms. Pak 
paid most of the rent, often with her own funds, and that Norris 
contributed $15,000. (SOD, pp. 23:16–25; 28:17–23.) These findings negate 
any theory that Ms. Pak caused Brown to incur rent liability.

The Court further found that Brown consented to the office-sharing 
arrangement, required Ms. Pak to use the office to finish his remaining 
cases, and accepted the arrangement. (SOD, pp. 23:4–26.) Consent bars 
liability. The SOD also found that Plaintiffs presented no evidence that 
Norris owed rent or office expenses. (SOD, pp. 23:4–25.) Without evidence 
of a rent obligation owed by anyone other than Brown, there is no basis for 
damages.

The SOD establishes that the lease was in Brown's name, that Brown ousted 
Ms. Pak and Norris from the office on October 21, 2023, and that after that 
date Ms. Pak had no ability to pay rent, negotiate, or communicate with the 
landlord. (SOD, pp. 23:4–26.) Any balloon or accelerated rent obligation 
was caused by Brown's unilateral termination of access—not by Ms. Pak. 
Given Brown's nonpayment, Ms. Pak's substantial rent contributions, and 
Norris's $15,000 contribution, any alleged $24,000 obligation is fully 
offset under the SOD's own findings. California law requires damages to be 
reduced by any benefit conferred. Oasis West Realty, LLC, 51 Cal.4th at 820.

Because the SOD found no wrongful taking, no misuse of funds, no causation, 
and no harm, the damages awards cannot stand. A judgment that awards 
damages without supporting findings must be vacated under Code of Civil 
Procedure §663."

stated intentions. Conduct undertaken with the principal's knowledge and in

breach of fiduciary duty. Van de Kamp v. Bank of America, 204 Cal.App.3d 
819, 857 (1988).
