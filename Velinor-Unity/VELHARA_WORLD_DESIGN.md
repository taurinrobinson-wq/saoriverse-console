# Velhara: Post-Collapse World Design

> *A world without tools is one thing. A world without the knowledge to use them is something else entirely.*

---

## ⭐ Core Philosophy

Velhara is not a medieval world. It is not a post-apocalyptic wasteland. It is a **post-identity civilization**.

### The Collapse Was Not Physical

**People did not lose tools.**

They lost **coherence.**

They lost **identity.**

They lost the **procedural memory** that makes culture reproducible.

This distinction defines everything about how life works in Velhara:

- They can still **use** objects → They cannot **reproduce** complex systems
- They can still **recognize** shelter → They cannot **organize** large-scale construction
- They can still **wear** clothing → They cannot **manufacture** textiles at scale
- They can still **hunt** or **gather** → They cannot **build agriculture** without rediscovery

### The Result

**Velhara is a world without continuity.**

Material abundance exists. Knowledge does not. Skills can be rediscovered through embodied memory, but systems cannot be rebuilt from first principles. This creates a world that is visually modern, emotionally ancient, materially hybrid, psychologically fragmented, and socially emergent.

---

## Architecture & Habitation

### Where Do They Live?

**They live in the shells of the old world — but simplified, repurposed, and half-forgotten.**

#### Common Dwellings

- Abandoned apartments with improvised repairs
- Office buildings with vines growing through them
- Train stations turned into communal shelters
- Shipping containers repurposed as homes
- Rooftop gardens grown by trial and error
- Tents and tarps inside larger structures
- Stairwells converted into living spaces
- Parking garages turned into markets
- Warehouses subdivided into family units
- Bridges retrofitted with living structures

#### Visual Characteristics

- Multiple layers of repair visible at once
- Mix of modern materials (sheet metal, plastic, glass) with organic elements (wood, rope, cloth)
- Signs of adaptive reuse: staircases leading nowhere, rooms repurposed for storage
- Plants growing into and around structures
- Weathering patterns that reveal the original use of spaces
- Safety that feels improvised rather than engineered

### What New Construction Exists?

They **cannot** build:

- Houses
- Masonry structures
- Complex joinery
- Metalworking facilities
- Textiles at industrial scale
- Engineered structures

They **can** build:

- Simple lean-tos
- Wooden frames held together with rope or crude nails
- Basic furniture (tables, stools, benches)
- Fire pits and smoke structures
- Drying racks for food
- Simple clay pots
- Crude woven mats
- Fencing and barriers

### Embodied Memory & Specialization

**Some individuals rediscover skills through embodied memory** — not through intellectual understanding, but through muscle memory and intuition.

- A former carpenter cannot explain joinery, but their hands can still make a sturdy table
- A former seamstress cannot design clothing, but can stitch repairs perfectly
- A former engineer cannot rebuild infrastructure, but can rig a working pulley system
- A former mason recognizes stone patterns and can repair walls, but cannot explain the principles

This gives Velhara **NPC specialization without civilization**. People are useful not because they understand systems, but because their bodies remember fragments of skill.

## Material Culture & Aesthetics

### Clothing & Personal Items

The visual signature of Velhara is **post-memory modern tribal**.

#### What They Wear

- Mismatched layers (form follows function, not fashion)
- Jackets tied closed with rope where zippers fail
- Shirts with hand-stitched patches in contrasting materials
- Pants cut into shorts or cargo-style adaptations
- Scarves repurposed as belts or head wraps
- Blankets worn as cloaks
- Bits of old uniforms still recognizable
- Found fabrics draped over the body, not tailored
- Leather scraped from old furniture repurposed as straps
- Dyes from natural sources creating unpredictable patterns

#### What They Don't Have

- New textiles (weaving is too complex to rediscover)
- Professionally tanned leather
- Consistent fashion or style
- Industrial production of anything
- Modern synthetic fabrics (old stocks are scavenged and reused)

#### What They Do Have

- Repair culture (visible mending is normal)
- Repurposing expertise (recognizing when something can be used differently)
- Adaptive techniques (wrapping, tying, layering)
- Improvisation as an aesthetic
- Personal customization through wear and modification

### Food Storage & Cooking

#### Available Methods

- Old refrigerators repurposed as cabinets and storage boxes
- Plastic bins and metal shelves scavenged from old stores
- Wooden crates and clay pots (partially rediscovered)
- Woven baskets (rediscovered through trial)
- Rooftop and ground-level gardens (small-scale, trial-and-error agriculture)
- Drying racks for meat, fish, and plants
- Smoking pits for preservation
- Communal storage rooms in larger structures
- Root cellars in basements and underground areas

#### Cooking & Preparation

- Open fires and fire pits (primary heat source)
- Salvaged metal pans and cooking implements
- Clay vessels for boiling
- Flat stones for cooking
- Communal kitchens in shared spaces
- **No electricity** (except rare, fragile salvaged systems)
- **No industrial manufacturing** of food or tools

#### Reality of Food

- Storage is local, small-scale, and fragile
- Seasonal variation is extreme
- Spoilage happens often
- Trade and barter are essential survival tools
- "Bad harvest years" can mean starvation
- Successful food production creates social status

## Economy & Skill-Based Society

### Subsistence Model

Velhara operates as a **scavenger-forager society with emerging proto-skills** — not fully regressed, not fully reinvented, existing in the liminal space between.

#### Sources of Food & Materials

- Foraging from wild areas
- Fishing (rivers, lakes, coastal areas)
- Small-scale animal trapping
- Trial-and-error agriculture (gardens and small fields)
- Scavenging canned and packaged goods from old stores
- Rediscovery of simple agriculture techniques through embodied knowledge
- Trade and barter within local communities

#### What This Means

- They are **not nomadic** (they inhabit old structures)
- They are **not tribal in the primitive sense** (they remember technology exists)
- They are **not fully regressed** (old knowledge fragments emerge)
- They are **not fully reinvented** (they haven't solved the basic problems again)

### Social Structure

#### Specialization Without Hierarchy

People gain status and role through:

1. **Embodied Skills**: Those who remember how to fix things, grow food, repair structures
2. **Knowledge Fragments**: Those who understand pieces of the old world
3. **Community Maintenance**: Those who keep shared spaces functional
4. **Memory Keepers**: Those who tell stories of the before-time (whether accurate or mythologized)
5. **Mediators & Traders**: Those who facilitate exchange between groups

#### Conflict Points

- Control of resources (particularly food storage areas)
- Access to tools and salvageable materials
- Knowledge conflicts (disagreement about what the old world was)
- Personality and charisma (TONE stats play a role here)
- Grievances and old memories (some NPCs carry grudges)

## Visual Design Implications

### Color Palette

- Earth tones from materials (rust, clay, weathered wood, moss green)
- Faded modern colors (bleached blues, grayed blacks, worn plastics)
- Accent colors from natural dyes and preserved textiles
- Visible decay: browns, greens, grays from weathering
- Organic intrusion: ivy green, fungal blacks, water stains

### Texture & Material Language

- Raw wood lashed with rope
- Metal showing rust and patina
- Concrete broken and repaired with incompatible materials
- Glass still present but often broken or obscured
- Fabric worn thin and repeatedly patched
- Clay pots showing hand-marks
- Natural growth (plants, moss, vines) integrated into structures

### What's Visible in a Scene

- **Evidence of repair**: Visible patches, replacements, different materials joined together
- **Evidence of reuse**: Old signs still on buildings, purpose changed but history visible
- **Evidence of scavenging**: Mismatched materials, objects from different eras combined
- **Evidence of adaptation**: Makeshift solutions that work but look improvised
- **Evidence of time**: Weathering, decay, organic growth

### What's NOT Visible

- Modern manufacturing
- Clean, uniform construction
- Contemporary fashion
- Industrial efficiency
- Planned urban design (it evolved through occupation, not planning)
- Technology (except broken/salvaged pieces)

## NPC Design Connection

### How This Shapes Characters

Each NPC embodies a fragment of pre-collapse memory combined with post-collapse adaptation:

- **Ravi** (merchant): Remembers commerce, tries to maintain trade networks
- **Nima** (observer): Recalls patterns and systems, tries to understand the collapse
- **Kaelen** (mediator): Holds grudges and memories, navigates social conflict
- **Tovren** (skeptic): Questions narratives, holds alternative truths
- **Vex** (rebel): Rejects the old world, tries to build something new

Their interactions are shaped by:

1. What fragments of the old world they remember
2. How they adapt to post-collapse reality
3. How they resolve cognitive dissonance between memory and reality
4. How they cooperate or conflict over resources and narrative

## World Rules & Consistency

### Hard Constraints

1. **No electricity** (except fragile, localized salvage projects)
2. **No manufacturing** of complex items
3. **No agriculture at scale** (gardens only)
4. **No writing systems** (memory culture is oral/embodied)
5. **No mass production** (everything is handmade, salvaged, or inherited)

### Soft Realities

1. Some skills can be rediscovered through embodied memory
2. Old materials can be repurposed creatively
3. Knowledge fragments exist but are often mythologized
4. Danger comes from unpredictability (forgotten systems can trap the curious)
5. Communities are small and interdependent

### Potential Conflicts with Progress

- Someone might want to "rebuild" civilization (generates conflict)
- Someone might have knowledge that threatens the status quo (generates conflict)
- Resource scarcity creates competition (generates conflict)
- Memory disagreements about "what was" (generates conflict, roleplay, NPC interactions)

---

## Design Checklist for Scenes & NPCs

When creating new areas or characters in Velhara, ask:

- ✓ Is this built from old structures or new improvisation?
- ✓ What materials are visible? Do they show repair and reuse?
- ✓ What skills were needed to create this? Are they rediscovered or ongoing?
- ✓ What would break first? What would last longest?
- ✓ What does this NPC remember about the old world?
- ✓ How does that memory shape their current role?
- ✓ What conflict could arise from their knowledge/role?
- ✓ What do they need that only others can provide?
- ✓ How would they respond if the player disrupts their world?

---

## Conclusion

Velhara is a world defined by **loss of continuity, not loss of material**.

It is visually modern but emotionally archaic.

It is materially abundant but systemically fragmented.

It is socially emergent but psychologically haunted by memory.

This tension — between the world that was and the world that is — is where all conflict, character, and narrative possibility lives.

**The architecture is the character. The decay is the story. The adaptation is the hope.**
